🌿 How this plan works

In her condition (IAS), the real danger is her blood sugar dropping suddenly — a few hours after meals and overnight. Everything here keeps it steady, avoiding both lows and big spikes.

1
Eat small amounts often. Never skip a meal or go long without food — that's why there are 6 meals a day.
2
Protein + slow carbs together. Pair egg, fish, dal or kollu with millets or a little rice. They slow sugar release.
3
No fast sugar alone. Avoid sweets, sugary drinks and large plain-rice portions — they spike, then crash.
🥣 The bedtime cornstarch
🌙 Every night before bed

Mix 1–2 tablespoons of plain cornstarch (the fine white "cornflour") into water or milk and give it raw — without sugar. It releases glucose slowly overnight and prevents the early-morning low. About 30 g holds sugar steady for ~7 hours. Confirm the exact amount with her doctor.

💊 Steroid taper (as the doctor advised)

Morning stays at 60 mg. The night dose drops by 10 mg each week until it reaches 0 — then back to Amrita for review. Watch more carefully for lows in the days after each reduction.

Always follow the doctor's latest instructions — the morning-dose plan will be decided at the Amrita review. Set the start date below.

🐟 Her protein guide

Fish (best, 3–4×/week): small oily fish like mathi (sardine) & ayala (mackerel) — great for cholesterol and joints.

Eggs (daily): easy complete protein — boiled, podimas, or omelette with little oil.

Goat (1–2×/week, lean) and chicken (max 2×/week, small, not fried). Chicken is kept low — she finds it "heaty." Always swappable for fish, egg, or a veg protein.

❤️ For cholesterol & joints

Cholesterol: choose fish & vegetable proteins over red meat; cook light (curry/steam, not deep-fry); plenty of fibre — greens, millets, dal, oats; garlic, oats and nuts help.

Arthritis (knees, and the waist/stomach spread she mentioned): oily fish (omega-3), turmeric, ginger and garlic ease inflammation; colourful vegetables and fruit help; staying at a healthy weight eases the knees.

Her lows are mostly reactive — they come a few hours after a meal (especially a carb-heavy one) and overnight, rather than from fasting alone.

🍽️ Her food approach

The whole diet is built to flatten the after-meal sugar spike, because it's that spike-then-crash that drives her lows.

  • Protein with every meal (egg, dal, kollu, fish, paneer, curd).
  • Slow carbs — millets, a little rice, whole wheat — never fast sugar on its own.
  • Plenty of greens and vegetables for fibre.
  • Small amounts, often; never long gaps without food.
  • Bedtime cornstarch for the overnight stretch.

Foods like amla, jamun and bitter gourd gently support her sugar and are good as food, in small amounts, with a meal — but never as concentrated juices, powders or extracts, which can lower sugar too strongly.

📈 What we've tracked
🔬 About IAS (the science)
📖 What it is

Insulin Autoimmune Syndrome (IAS), or Hirata disease, is a rare autoimmune cause of low blood sugar, first described by Dr Yukimasa Hirata in Japan in 1970. The body makes antibodies against its own insulin.

Doctors define it by four things: low blood sugar with high insulin levels, high insulin-autoantibody titres, no history of insulin injections, and a normal pancreas (no tumour). It tends to appear after age 40.

🎢 Why her sugar drops (the roller-coaster)

After a meal, the antibodies grab onto her insulin so it can't act normally straight away — sugar may even rise a little, which prompts the body to make still more insulin.

A few hours later, the antibodies let go of all that stored insulin at once, regardless of what the sugar is doing — causing a delayed crash, typically some hours after eating and overnight. The lingering insulin also blunts the body's normal recovery, so the low lasts longer.

This is exactly why her plan works: small low-GI meals avoid the big spike that starts the cycle, and bedtime cornstarch keeps glucose trickling in overnight.

🧬 Who gets it & why

There's a strong genetic link to a tissue type called HLA-DRB1*04:06, which is common in Japanese and Korean populations — so IAS is far more frequently reported in East Asia. In Western countries it's rarer and tends to be linked to a related type (DRB1*04:03).

It often appears alongside other autoimmune conditions (such as Graves' disease or rheumatoid arthritis), or is set off by a viral infection or a triggering medication acting on that genetic background.

💊 Drugs & supplements that can trigger it

More than half of cases follow a drug containing a sulfhydryl (thiol) group. These can "unzip" the insulin molecule and expose it to the immune system, prompting the antibodies — especially in genetically susceptible people.

Well-documented triggers include methimazole / carbimazole (thyroid drugs), alpha-lipoic acid (thioctic acid — a common over-the-counter supplement), penicillamine, captopril and clopidogrel. More recent reports add proton-pump inhibitors and some diabetes medicines.

This is the reason her avoid-list below matters so much.

🔍 How it's diagnosed

Blood taken during or after a low shows high insulin together with high C-peptide, and — the key finding — high insulin-autoantibody titres, in someone who has never injected insulin.

Doctors rule out an insulinoma (a tumour) with imaging and check that no sulfonylurea diabetes drug is involved. Specialised lab steps (PEG precipitation and gel chromatography) confirm that the insulin is bound to antibodies. A tell-tale clue is insulin being disproportionately high compared with C-peptide.

🩺 How it's treated

Most cases are self-limiting and settle on their own. The first line is diet — small, frequent, low-GI meals, avoiding simple sugars, with plenty of fibre — to blunt the after-meal spike. Any trigger drug is stopped. Raw cornstarch helps prevent lows, especially overnight, and acarbose (which slows carbohydrate absorption) can help.

More severe cases use corticosteroids (which reduce both the hypoglycaemia and the antibody levels), sometimes diazoxide or somatostatin analogues to lower insulin release, and rarely immunosuppressants (azathioprine, rituximab) or plasma exchange. Amma's plan — diet + cornstarch + a steroid taper — follows this approach.

🌅 The outlook

Generally good. Many people improve within months once triggers are removed and the immune flare settles — though the antibodies themselves can take longer (sometimes a couple of years) to fully disappear. Day-to-day care is about preventing lows while her body recovers, which is exactly what this app is for.

🚫 Must-avoid for her

Drugs and supplements in the sulfhydryl / antioxidant group that can trigger or worsen IAS:

  • Alpha-lipoic acid (thioctic acid)
  • NAC, cysteine, methionine, glutathione supplements
  • Methimazole / carbimazole
  • Captopril · hydralazine
  • Clopidogrel · penicillamine

Also avoid concentrated sugar-lowering remedies that can stack onto her low risk: bitter-gourd (karela) juice, jamun-seed powder, amla extract capsules, large fenugreek doses.

Always tell any new doctor she has IAS before she starts a new medicine.

🩸 If her sugar is low

What to do, step by step

Warning signs: shakiness, sweating, sudden hunger, dizziness, fast heartbeat, weakness, irritability, confusion, blurred vision, feeling faint. The CGM will also show it.

The "15–15" steps

1
Give ~15 g fast sugar: ½ cup fruit juice, OR 3–4 teaspoons sugar/glucose in water, OR a few glucose biscuits.
2
Wait 15 minutes and recheck her sugar (CGM or glucometer).
3
If still low, repeat. Once recovered, give a small snack with protein — a boiled egg and a few nuts, or curd — to hold it steady.
Emergency: if she is drowsy, confused, or cannot swallow safely, do not force food or water — get medical help immediately.
